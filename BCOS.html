📌 Unit 1: Introduction to Entrepreneurship

▼

1.1 Meaning and Definition of Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship is the process of designing, launching, and running a new business, which typically begins as a small business or startup, offering a product, process, or service for sale or hire. The word entrepreneur originates from the French word "entreprendre" which means "to undertake." An entrepreneur is an individual who undertakes the risk of starting a new business venture, organizes the factors of production, and coordinates them to create value.

According to Joseph Schumpeter (1934), an entrepreneur is an innovator who introduces new combinations of means of production. He described entrepreneurship as a process of "creative destruction" where old industries are replaced by new ones through innovation.

Peter Drucker defined entrepreneurship as a systematic innovation that consists of purposeful and organized search for changes, and the systematic analysis of opportunities such changes might offer for economic or social innovation.

The Government of India (MSME Act, 2006) defines an entrepreneur as a person who establishes and manages a business enterprise, organizes factors of production, and bears the risk involved in the business.

Key Definition for Exams: Entrepreneurship is the capacity and willingness to develop, organize, and manage a business venture along with any of its risks to make a profit. The most visible form of entrepreneurship is the starting of new businesses.

Mnemonic: "RISK-OP" — Remember the core elements of entrepreneurship:

Risk bearing · Innovation · Skill (managerial) · Knowledge application · Opportunity recognition · Profit motive

1.2 Characteristics of Entrepreneurship

  1. Innovation: Entrepreneurship involves introducing new products, methods, markets, raw materials, or organizational structures.
  2. Risk Bearing: The entrepreneur assumes the uncertainty of future outcomes and bears both financial and psychological risks.
  3. Profit Potential: The primary motivation is earning profit; profit is the reward for risk-taking and innovation.
  4. Organization of Resources: Entrepreneurs mobilize land, labor, capital, and technology to create productive enterprises.
  5. Decision Making: Entrepreneurs make critical decisions under conditions of uncertainty regarding product, price, location, promotion, and production.
  6. Economic Activity: Entrepreneurship involves the creation and operation of an enterprise aimed at value creation and economic gain.
  7. Gap Filling: Entrepreneurs identify gaps in the market and fill them with appropriate goods or services.
  8. Dynamic Process: Entrepreneurship adapts to changing economic, social, and technological environments.

1.3 Types of Entrepreneurs

Based on Clarence Danhof's Classification:

Imitative EntrepreneursCopy existing successful ideas; adapt to local conditionsLocal franchise owners

Other Classifications:

  • By Scale: Small-scale, Medium-scale, Large-scale entrepreneurs
  • By Sector: Agricultural, Industrial, Service, Trading entrepreneurs
  • By Gender: Women entrepreneurs (a growing focus in India with schemes like Stand-Up India)
  • By Motivation: Opportunity-driven vs. Necessity-driven entrepreneurs
  • Social Entrepreneurs: Focus on solving social problems rather than profit maximization
  • Serial Entrepreneurs: Individuals who start multiple businesses over time
  • Technopreneurs: Technology-focused entrepreneurs in IT, biotech, etc.

1.5 Role of Entrepreneurship in Economic Development

  • Employment Generation: New ventures create jobs. MSMEs in India employ over 110 million people.
  • Wealth Creation: Successful enterprises generate wealth for founders, employees, and investors.
  • Regional Development: Industries in backward areas reduce regional imbalances.
  • GDP Growth: Entrepreneurial activity contributes significantly to national income.
  • Innovation & Technology: Entrepreneurs drive technological progress and productivity improvements.
  • Export Promotion: Many startups contribute to foreign exchange earnings.
  • Social Change: Entrepreneurship empowers marginalized groups and promotes inclusive growth.

📌 Unit 2: Entrepreneurial Development

▼

2.1 Concept of Entrepreneurial Development

Entrepreneurial Development (ED) refers to the process of enhancing entrepreneurial skills, knowledge, and capabilities through structured training programs, institutional support, and policy interventions. The goal is to develop individuals into competent entrepreneurs who can start and manage successful business ventures. ED encompasses personality development, skill enhancement, motivation building, and creating a supportive ecosystem.

In India, entrepreneurial development gained momentum after the establishment of institutions like the Entrepreneurship Development Institute of India (EDII) in Ahmedabad (1983), the National Institute for Entrepreneurship and Small Business Development (NIESBUD), and various state-level training centers.

Mnemonic: "ED-SKILL" — Components of Entrepreneurial Development:

Education & training · Development of personality · Support systems · Knowledge transfer · Infrastructure provision · Leadership building · Legal & policy framework

2.2 Need and Importance of Entrepreneurial Development

  • Employment Generation: Developing entrepreneurs leads to job creation at scale.
  • Balanced Regional Development: ED programs in backward areas reduce regional disparities.
  • Optimal Resource Utilization: Trained entrepreneurs efficiently utilize local resources.
  • Innovation Promotion: ED fosters a culture of innovation and problem-solving.
  • Economic Self-Reliance: Reduces dependence on government jobs and foreign companies.
  • Social Inclusion: ED programs for SC/ST, women, and rural populations promote inclusive growth.
  • Export Competitiveness: Quality-focused entrepreneurs enhance export potential.

2.3 Entrepreneurial Development Process

  1. Identification of Potential Entrepreneurs: Through surveys, psychometric tests, and interviews to find individuals with entrepreneurial traits like need for achievement, risk-taking ability, and creativity.
  2. Motivational Training: Building self-confidence, achievement motivation (based on McClelland's theory), and entrepreneurial spirit.
  3. Skill Development: Technical skills, managerial skills, financial literacy, marketing knowledge, and digital skills.
  4. Project Preparation: Training on business plan writing, feasibility analysis, and project report preparation.
  5. Institutional Support: Connecting trainees with banks, DICs, MSME centers, and mentorship networks.
  6. Follow-up and Handholding: Post-training support, troubleshooting, and mentoring for sustainability.

2.4 Entrepreneurship Development Programs (EDPs) in India

EDPs are structured training programs designed to develop entrepreneurial competencies. Key institutions conducting EDPs include:

  • EDII (Entrepreneurship Development Institute of India): Ahmedabad-based national institute offering comprehensive EDPs.
  • NIESBUD: National Institute for Entrepreneurship and Small Business Development, Noida.
  • IIE (Indian Institute of Entrepreneurship): Guwahati-based, focusing on North-East India.
  • SIDBI: Small Industries Development Bank of India provides financial and training support.
  • State-level ED Cells: District Industries Centers (DICs) conduct local EDPs.
  • NIMSME: National Institute for Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises, Hyderabad.

2.5 Theories of Entrepreneurship

McClelland's Need for Achievement (nAch) Theory: David McClelland proposed that individuals with a high need for achievement are more likely to become entrepreneurs. High nAch individuals set challenging but achievable goals, take calculated risks, and seek feedback on performance. McClelland's Kakinada Experiment in Andhra Pradesh demonstrated that achievement motivation can be developed through training.

Hagen's Status Withdrawal Theory: Everett Hagen proposed that entrepreneurship emerges when a social group experiences status withdrawal or loss of social standing, motivating them to seek economic achievement as an alternative path.

Cochran's Theory: Thomas Cochran emphasized that entrepreneurship is influenced by cultural values, social sanctions, and role expectations within a society.

Exam Focus: McClelland's nAch theory is frequently asked. Remember: High nAch → calculated risk-taking → entrepreneurial behavior. The Kakinada experiment proved nAch is trainable.

2.6 Challenges in Entrepreneurial Development

  • Lack of adequate funding and venture capital access
  • Complex regulatory environment and bureaucratic hurdles
  • Inadequate infrastructure (power, transport, internet)
  • Social and cultural barriers (fear of failure, family pressure for secure jobs)
  • Skill gaps in technology, management, and finance
  • Limited market access for small producers
  • Gender bias affecting women entrepreneurs

📌 Unit 3: Business Opportunity Identification & Planning

▼

3.1 Business Opportunity Identification

Business opportunity identification is the process of discovering and evaluating potential business ideas that can be converted into viable commercial ventures. It is the foundation of entrepreneurship. An opportunity exists where there is an unmet need, a problem to solve, or a gap in the market that can be profitably addressed.

According to Peter Drucker, opportunities arise from: (1) The unexpected, (2) Incongruities, (3) Process needs, (4) Industry and market changes, (5) Demographic changes, (6) Changes in perception, and (7) New knowledge.

Mnemonic: "SWOT-PEST" — Framework for opportunity scanning:

Strengths · Weaknesses · Opportunities · Threats — plus — Political · Economic · Social · Technological factors

3.2 Sources of Business Ideas

  • Personal Experience: Problems faced in daily life can spark business ideas.
  • Market Research: Systematic study of market gaps and consumer preferences.
  • Government Policies: New schemes and subsidies create opportunities (e.g., solar energy push).
  • Technological Advancements: AI, IoT, blockchain, and digital platforms open new avenues.
  • Trade Fairs and Exhibitions: Exposure to new products and trends.
  • Franchise Opportunities: Established brands offering franchise partnerships.
  • Import Substitution: Producing goods locally that are currently imported.
  • Social Media Trends: Viral products and changing consumer behaviors.

3.3 Feasibility Analysis

Before committing resources, an entrepreneur must conduct a thorough feasibility study covering:

  1. Market Feasibility: Demand assessment, target market size, competition analysis, pricing strategy.
  2. Technical Feasibility: Availability of technology, raw materials, machinery, skilled labor, and infrastructure.
  3. Financial Feasibility: Capital requirements, projected revenues, break-even analysis, ROI, and funding sources.
  4. Legal Feasibility: Compliance with laws, licenses required, environmental clearances, and regulatory approvals.
  5. Operational Feasibility: Day-to-day operations, supply chain, logistics, and human resource requirements.

Exam Tip: A detailed feasibility report is often the deciding factor for investors and banks when evaluating loan applications. Always cover all five feasibility dimensions in your answer.

3.4 Business Plan Preparation

A business plan is a comprehensive written document that describes the business idea, objectives, strategies, market analysis, financial projections, and operational roadmap. It serves as a blueprint for the venture and a communication tool for stakeholders.

Key Components of a Business Plan:

  1. Executive Summary: A concise overview of the entire plan (written last but placed first).
  2. Company Description: Mission, vision, legal structure, location, and history.
  3. Product/Service Description: Features, benefits, unique selling proposition (USP), and stage of development.
  4. Market Analysis: Industry overview, target market, competitor analysis, and market share projections.
  5. Marketing & Sales Strategy: Pricing, promotion, distribution channels, and sales tactics.
  6. Operations Plan: Production process, facilities, equipment, suppliers, and quality control.
  7. Management & Organization: Key team members, their roles, and organizational structure.
  8. Financial Plan: Start-up costs, income statement, cash flow projection, balance sheet, and break-even analysis.
  9. Appendices: Supporting documents like market research data, resumes, legal documents.

3.5 Project Formulation and Report

A project report is a detailed document prepared for securing financing from banks and financial institutions. It includes technical details, cost estimates, means of finance, profitability projections, and debt service coverage. In India, project reports must align with guidelines from institutions like SIDBI, NABARD, and commercial banks.

📌 Unit 4: Financial Management for Entrepreneurs

▼

4.1 Meaning and Importance of Financial Management

Financial management is the strategic planning, organizing, directing, and controlling of financial activities such as procurement and utilization of funds. For entrepreneurs, effective financial management is critical because inadequate capitalization and poor cash flow management are among the top reasons for startup failures. It involves decisions about capital budgeting, capital structure, working capital management, and dividend policy.

Mnemonic: "3C-FAME" — Key financial management areas:

Capital budgeting · Capital structure · Cash flow · Financial planning · Asset management · Monitoring · Expense control

4.2 Sources of Finance for Entrepreneurs

A) Owned Funds (Equity):

  • Personal Savings: The most common initial source; demonstrates commitment to investors.
  • Friends and Family: Informal funding at early stages; often called "love money."
  • Angel Investors: High-net-worth individuals who invest in early-stage startups in exchange for equity.
  • Venture Capital: Professional investment firms funding high-growth potential startups.
  • Private Equity: Larger investments in more mature companies.
  • Crowdfunding: Raising small amounts from many people via online platforms.

B) Borrowed Funds (Debt):

  • Bank Loans: Term loans, overdrafts, cash credit from commercial banks.
  • SIDBI Loans: Specialized financing for MSMEs through schemes like SMILE, CGTMSE.
  • NABARD: Refinance for agricultural and rural enterprises.
  • MUDRA Loans: Micro Units Development & Refinance Agency offers loans up to ₹10 lakh under Shishu, Kishor, and Tarun categories.
  • NBFCs: Non-banking financial companies offer more flexible terms.
  • Government Subsidies: Various central and state government schemes provide capital subsidies.

4.3 Working Capital Management

Working capital is the capital required for day-to-day operations. It is calculated as Current Assets minus Current Liabilities. Proper working capital management ensures the business can meet its short-term obligations and operate smoothly.

Components: Cash management, inventory management, receivables management, and payables management. The operating cycle (cash → inventory → sales → receivables → cash) determines working capital needs.

4.4 Break-Even Analysis

The break-even point (BEP) is the level of sales at which total revenue equals total costs (both fixed and variable), resulting in neither profit nor loss. It is a critical tool for entrepreneurs to determine the minimum sales volume needed to cover costs.

Formula: BEP (in units) = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price per Unit − Variable Cost per Unit)

Margin of Safety: Actual Sales − Break-Even Sales. Higher margin indicates lower risk.

Exam Focus: Practice numerical problems on break-even analysis. Questions often ask to calculate BEP, margin of safety, and target profit sales volume.

4.5 Financial Statements and Ratios

Entrepreneurs must understand and analyze three key financial statements:

  • Income Statement (Profit & Loss): Shows revenue, expenses, and profit over a period.
  • Balance Sheet: Snapshot of assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time.
  • Cash Flow Statement: Tracks cash inflows and outflows from operations, investing, and financing.

Key Ratios: Current Ratio (liquidity), Debt-Equity Ratio (leverage), Gross Profit Ratio (profitability), Inventory Turnover Ratio (efficiency).

📌 Unit 5: Marketing for Entrepreneurs

5.1 Marketing Concepts for Small Business

Marketing is the process of identifying, anticipating, and satisfying customer needs profitably. For entrepreneurs and small businesses, marketing is especially critical because they often lack the brand recognition of established competitors. Effective marketing enables a startup to create awareness, attract customers, and build loyalty with limited resources.

The marketing mix (4Ps) — Product, Price, Place, Promotion — must be carefully designed. Modern marketing extends to 7Ps for services: adding People, Process, and Physical Evidence.

Mnemonic: "STP-4P" — Marketing strategy framework:

Segmentation → Targeting → Positioning → then apply 4Ps (Product, Price, Place, Promotion)

5.2 Market Research for Entrepreneurs

Market research is the systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of data about a target market. Methods include surveys, focus groups, interviews, observation, and secondary data analysis. For entrepreneurs, low-cost methods like Google Forms surveys, social media polls, and competitor analysis are practical starting points.

5.3 Digital Marketing for Small Business

  • Social Media Marketing: Leveraging platforms like Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn, and YouTube.
  • Search Engine Optimization (SEO): Improving website visibility on Google.
  • Content Marketing: Creating valuable blogs, videos, and infographics to attract customers.
  • Email Marketing: Building and nurturing customer relationships through newsletters.
  • WhatsApp Business: Direct communication and catalog sharing for micro-enterprises.
  • Google My Business: Free listing for local businesses to appear on Google Maps and search.

5.4 Branding and Positioning

Branding is creating a unique identity through name, logo, design, and messaging. Positioning is how the brand is perceived in the customer's mind relative to competitors. A strong USP (Unique Selling Proposition) is essential for differentiation.

5.5 Sales and Distribution Channels

Distribution channels include direct sales (online store, company website), retail partnerships, wholesalers, distributors, e-commerce marketplaces (Amazon, Flipkart, Meesho), and franchise models. The choice depends on product type, target market, and capital availability.

6.1 Definition and Classification of Small Business in India

As per the MSMED Act, 2006 (amended in 2020), enterprises are classified based on investment in plant & machinery/equipment and annual turnover:

CategoryInvestment LimitAnnual Turnover Limit
Micro EnterpriseUp to ₹1 croreUp to ₹5 crore
Small EnterpriseUp to ₹10 croreUp to ₹50 crore
Medium EnterpriseUp to ₹50 croreUp to ₹250 crore

Exam Update: The 2020 revision merged manufacturing and service enterprises under uniform criteria. Earlier definitions (pre-2020) had separate limits for manufacturing and services.

6.2 Role and Importance of MSMEs in India

  • Contribute approximately 30% of India's GDP and 45% of exports
  • Employ over 110 million people across 63+ million enterprises
  • Promote inclusive growth by providing opportunities in rural and semi-urban areas
  • Foster innovation through low-cost, flexible production methods
  • Serve as ancillary units to large industries, strengthening supply chains
  • Support traditional arts, crafts, and indigenous knowledge systems

6.3 Problems Faced by Small Businesses

  1. Financial Problems: Difficulty accessing formal credit, high interest rates, delayed payments from buyers.
  2. Marketing Problems: Limited brand recognition, competition from large firms and imports.
  3. Technology Obsolescence: Inability to afford modern machinery and digital tools.
  4. Regulatory Burden: Multiple compliances, licensing requirements, and inspection systems.
  5. Infrastructure Deficits: Unreliable power supply, poor transport connectivity in rural areas.
  6. Skilled Labor Shortage: Difficulty attracting and retaining qualified workers.
  7. Global Competition: Pressure from cheaper imports and organized retail chains.

6.4 Sickness in Small Business

Sickness refers to a condition where a business unit fails to generate surplus and struggles to meet financial obligations. Causes include managerial deficiencies, inadequate working capital, market downturns, technology obsolescence, and external shocks. Early warning signs include persistent cash losses, declining capacity utilization, and mounting overdue debts. Remedial measures include restructuring, financial support from banks, and management intervention.

📌 Unit 7: Legal Aspects of Business

▼

7.1 Forms of Business Organization

An entrepreneur must choose the appropriate legal structure for the venture. The main forms include:

  • Sole Proprietorship: Single owner, unlimited liability, minimal legal formalities, suitable for very small businesses.
  • Partnership: Two or more partners (maximum 50 as per Companies Act), governed by the Indian Partnership Act, 1932. Unlimited liability for partners.
  • Limited Liability Partnership (LLP): Combines benefits of partnership and limited liability; governed by LLP Act, 2008.
  • Private Limited Company: Minimum 2, maximum 200 members; shares cannot be publicly traded; limited liability.
  • Public Limited Company: Minimum 7 members, no maximum limit; can raise capital from the public.
  • One Person Company (OPC): Introduced by Companies Act 2013; single owner with limited liability.
  • Cooperative Society: Formed for mutual benefit of members; governed by Cooperative Societies Act.

Mnemonic: "SPLOP-CO" — Business structure options:

Sole Proprietorship · Partnership · LLP · OPC · Private Limited · Cooperative · Other (Public Ltd)

7.2 Business Registrations and Licenses

Key registrations required for businesses in India:

  • GST Registration: Mandatory for businesses with annual turnover above ₹40 lakh (goods) or ₹20 lakh (services).
  • MSME/Udyam Registration: Online registration for MSME benefits; free of cost.
  • Shop and Establishment Act License: State-level registration for commercial establishments.
  • FSSAI License: For food-related businesses.
  • Trade License: From local municipal authorities.
  • IEC (Import Export Code): For businesses engaged in international trade.
  • PAN and TAN: For tax compliance.

7.3 Intellectual Property Rights (IPR)

IPR protects creations of the mind. Key types include:

  • Patent: Protects inventions for 20 years; grants exclusive right to make, use, or sell.
  • Trademark: Protects brand names, logos, and slogans; renewable indefinitely.
  • Copyright: Protects literary, artistic, musical works; lifetime plus 60 years in India.
  • Design Registration: Protects the visual appearance of products.
  • Geographical Indication (GI): Protects products specific to a region (e.g., Darjeeling Tea, Kanchipuram Silk).

7.4 Key Business Laws

  • Companies Act, 2013: Governs incorporation, management, and dissolution of companies.
  • Indian Contract Act, 1872: Governs all commercial contracts.
  • Sale of Goods Act, 1930: Governs sale and purchase of movable goods.
  • Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881: Governs cheques, promissory notes, and bills of exchange.
  • Consumer Protection Act, 2019: Protects consumer rights; includes e-commerce provisions.
  • Labour Laws: Factories Act, Minimum Wages Act, ESI Act, EPF Act, etc.

8.1 Institutional Support System for Entrepreneurs

India has a comprehensive institutional framework to support entrepreneurship at central and state levels:

A) Central Level Institutions:

  • MSME Ministry: Nodal ministry for MSME policy and development.
  • SIDBI: Small Industries Development Bank of India — principal financial institution for MSMEs.
  • NABARD: National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development — supports rural enterprises.
  • NSIC: National Small Industries Corporation — provides marketing and technology support.
  • KVIC: Khadi and Village Industries Commission — promotes rural industries.
  • Coir Board: Supports coir industry enterprises.

B) State Level:

  • DICs (District Industries Centres): Single-window clearance and support at district level.
  • State Financial Corporations (SFCs): Provide term loans to small and medium enterprises.
  • State Industrial Development Corporations (SIDCs): Develop industrial infrastructure.

8.3 Role of DICs and SIDBI

District Industries Centres (DICs) serve as the primary interface between entrepreneurs and government at the district level. 📌 Unit 9: E-Commerce & Digital Entrepreneurship

▼

9.1 Concept of E-Commerce

E-Commerce (Electronic Commerce) refers to buying and selling goods or services using the internet, and the transfer of money and data to execute these transactions. It encompasses various models including B2B (Business-to-Business), B2C (Business-to-Consumer), C2C (Consumer-to-Consumer), B2G (Business-to-Government), and D2C (Direct-to-Consumer).

Mnemonic: "B2B2C-GD" — E-Commerce models:

B2B (Business to Business) · B2C (Business to Consumer) · C2C (Consumer to Consumer) · B2G (Business to Government) · D2C (Direct to Consumer)

9.2 Types of E-Commerce Models

  • B2B (Business-to-Business): Transactions between businesses. Example: IndiaMART, Alibaba. Involves bulk orders, negotiated pricing, and long-term contracts.
  • B2C (Business-to-Consumer): Businesses selling directly to end consumers. Example: Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra. Characterized by high volume, smaller order values, and emphasis on user experience.
  • C2C (Consumer-to-Consumer): Consumers selling to other consumers via platforms. Example: OLX, eBay, Facebook Marketplace.
  • D2C (Direct-to-Consumer): Manufacturers selling directly to consumers bypassing intermediaries. Example: Mamaearth, Boat Lifestyle, Lenskart.
  • Social Commerce: Selling through social media platforms like Instagram Shops, WhatsApp Business, and Facebook Marketplace.

9.3 Digital Entrepreneurship

Digital entrepreneurship involves creating and running a business primarily through digital platforms and technologies. It includes online retail, SaaS (Software as a Service), content creation, digital marketing agencies, ed-tech platforms, fintech startups, and app-based services. The barriers to entry are lower compared to traditional businesses, but competition is global and technology-driven.

Key enablers include widespread smartphone penetration, affordable internet (thanks to Jio revolution), UPI-based digital payments, and a growing ecosystem of logistics and cloud services.

9.4 E-Commerce Ecosystem in India

  • Marketplaces: Amazon India, Flipkart, Meesho, ShopClues
  • Payment Gateways: Razorpay, Paytm, PhonePe, Google Pay, Cashfree
  • Logistics: Delhivery, Shiprocket, Ecom Express, Blue Dart
  • Government Initiative: ONDC (Open Network for Digital Commerce) — aims to democratize e-commerce
  • Regulatory Framework: Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules, 2020; IT Act, 2000

9.5 Advantages and Challenges of E-Commerce

Advantages: Global reach, 24/7 availability, lower operational costs, personalized marketing, data-driven insights, scalability, and convenience for customers.

Challenges: Cybersecurity threats, data privacy concerns, intense competition, logistics and delivery issues, high return rates, digital divide in rural areas, and regulatory compliance.

📌 Unit 10: Social Entrepreneurship & Business Ethics

▼

10.1 Concept of Social Entrepreneurship

Social entrepreneurship is the practice of identifying social problems and using entrepreneurial principles to organize, create, and manage ventures that achieve social change. Unlike traditional entrepreneurs who focus primarily on profit, social entrepreneurs prioritize social impact while maintaining financial sustainability. The "triple bottom line" — People, Planet, Profit — guides social enterprises.

Examples in India: Amul (cooperative dairy empowering farmers), SEWA (Self Employed Women's Association), Aravind Eye Care (affordable eye surgery), Barefoot College (rural education and solar energy), Goonj (clothing and community development).

Mnemonic: "3P-SIM" — Social Entrepreneurship Framework:

3P (People, Planet, Profit) · Social mission · Innovation · Measurable impact

10.2 Characteristics of Social Entrepreneurs

  • Social Mission Driven: Primary focus on solving social, environmental, or community problems.
  • Innovative Approach: Develop novel solutions to persistent social challenges.
  • Sustainable Model: Generate revenue to ensure long-term viability without depending solely on donations.
  • Measurable Impact: Track and report social outcomes using metrics and frameworks.
  • Empowerment Focus: Enable communities to become self-reliant rather than creating dependency.
  • Scalability Mindset: Design solutions that can be replicated and scaled to reach more beneficiaries.

10.3 Business Ethics

Business ethics refers to the moral principles and standards that guide behavior in the world of business. It encompasses issues like honesty, integrity, fairness, transparency, accountability, and respect for stakeholders. Ethical businesses build trust, enhance reputation, and ensure long-term sustainability.

Key Ethical Principles:

  • Honesty: Truthful communication with customers, employees, and partners.
  • Fairness: Equitable treatment of all stakeholders; fair pricing and wages.
  • Transparency: Open disclosure of business practices, financial reporting, and product information.
  • Accountability: Taking responsibility for actions and their consequences.
  • Environmental Responsibility: Minimizing ecological footprint and promoting sustainability.
  • Consumer Protection: Ensuring product safety, quality, and honoring commitments.

10.4 Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

Under Section 135 of the Companies Act, 2013, companies meeting specified thresholds (net worth ≥₹500 crore, turnover ≥₹1000 crore, or net profit ≥₹5 crore) must spend at least 2% of average net profits of the preceding three years on CSR activities. Eligible activities include education, healthcare, poverty alleviation, environmental protection, and rural development.

Exam Tip: CSR is mandatory for qualifying companies under the Companies Act 2013. Remember the 2% rule and the qualifying criteria. This is a frequently tested topic.

10.5 Social Enterprises and Legal Structures in India

Social enterprises in India can be structured as:

  • Section 8 Company: Non-profit company under Companies Act 2013; profits used for promoting objectives.
  • Trust: Registered under Indian Trusts Act, 1882 for charitable purposes.
  • Society: Registered under Societies Registration Act, 1860.
  • Producer Company: Under Companies Act; for primary producers like farmers and artisans.
  • For-profit Social Enterprise: Private limited company with a social mission embedded in its operations.
