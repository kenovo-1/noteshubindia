📌 Unit 1: Nature and Scope of Applied Ethics

▼

1.1 What is Applied Ethics?

Applied Ethics is the branch of ethics that deals with the practical application of moral principles to real‑world situations. Unlike theoretical ethics (metaethics and normative ethics), which explores abstract questions about the nature of right and wrong, applied ethics directly addresses controversial moral issues in fields such as medicine, business, media, environment, and technology. It asks: "What ought we to do in this specific case?"

Mnemonic: "P‑B‑M‑E" — Major domains of Applied Ethics: Professional, Bioethics, Media, Environmental

1.2 Relationship with Normative Ethics

Applied ethics draws on normative ethical theories—utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics—to provide frameworks for decision‑making. For example, in bioethics, a utilitarian might support organ transplantation if it maximises overall welfare, while a deontologist would insist on the principle of informed consent regardless of outcome. Thus, applied ethics is the application of moral theories to specific dilemmas.

1.3 Methods in Applied Ethics

  • Case‑based reasoning (casuistry): Analysing particular cases and comparing them with paradigm cases.
  • Principle‑based approach: Applying broad moral principles (autonomy, beneficence, non‑maleficence, justice).
  • Reflective equilibrium: Coherence between specific judgements and general principles.

Exam Tip: Differentiate between metaethics, normative ethics, and applied ethics. Use examples like euthanasia or climate change to illustrate applied ethics.

📌 Unit 2: Ethical Theories and Their Applications

▼

2.1 Utilitarianism

Developed by Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, utilitarianism holds that the right action is the one that produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number. In applied contexts: cost‑benefit analysis in public policy, triage in disaster medicine. Act utilitarianism assesses each act; rule utilitarianism follows rules that maximise utility.

2.2 Deontology

Immanuel Kant's duty‑based ethics emphasises respect for persons and the Categorical Imperative: act only according to rules that could become universal laws, and treat humanity never merely as a means. Applications: informed consent in medicine, truth‑telling in media, prohibition on torture.

2.3 Virtue Ethics

Aristotle's approach focuses on character and virtues (honesty, courage, compassion) rather than rules or consequences. Applied virtue ethics asks: "What would a virtuous person do?" Used in professional ethics (e.g., a virtuous journalist, a compassionate doctor).

2.4 Care Ethics

Developed by Carol Gilligan, emphasises relationships, empathy, and responsibility towards vulnerable others. Important in nursing ethics, family ethics, and social work.

Mnemonic: "U‑D‑V‑C" — Theories: Utilitarian, Deontological, Virtue, Care

📌 Unit 3: Moral Reasoning and Argumentation

▼

3.1 Elements of Moral Reasoning

Moral reasoning involves identifying relevant facts, clarifying values, applying ethical principles, and constructing logical arguments. Key components: premises (factual and moral), inference, and conclusion. A sound moral argument is both valid (logical) and has true premises.

3.2 Logical Fallacies in Ethics

  • Naturalistic fallacy: Deriving 'ought' from 'is'.
  • Slippery slope: Exaggerating consequences.
  • Ad hominem: Attacking the person instead of argument.
  • False dilemma: Presenting only two extremes.

3.3 Moral Dilemmas and Resolution

Moral dilemmas arise when two or more ethical principles conflict. Resolution methods: ranking principles, specifying conditions, or using reflective equilibrium. Example: a doctor torn between patient confidentiality and public safety.

📌 Unit 4: Bioethics

▼

4.1 Definition and Scope

Bioethics examines ethical issues arising from advances in biology and medicine. It encompasses clinical ethics (doctor‑patient relationship), research ethics (human subjects protection), public health ethics (vaccination, quarantine), and emerging technologies (genetic engineering, AI in healthcare).

4.2 Key Principles (Beauchamp & Childress)

  • Autonomy: Respect for patient's decisions; informed consent.
  • Beneficence: Obligation to do good.
  • Non‑maleficence: Do not harm.
  • Justice: Fair distribution of healthcare resources.

4.3 End‑of‑Life Issues

Euthanasia (active vs. passive), physician‑assisted suicide, advance directives. Indian legal perspective: passive euthanasia permitted under strict guidelines (Aruna Shanbaug case, 2011; Common Cause vs. Union of India, 2018).

Exam Tip: Be familiar with the landmark Indian Supreme Court judgements on euthanasia and the concept of a living will.

📌 Unit 5: Environmental Ethics

▼

5.1 Anthropocentrism vs. Non‑anthropocentrism

Anthropocentrism assigns intrinsic value only to humans; nature has instrumental value. Non‑anthropocentrism extends moral consideration to animals (sentientism), all living beings (biocentrism), or ecosystems (ecocentrism). Deep ecology (Arne Naess) advocates for the intrinsic worth of all life forms.

5.2 Climate Change and Justice

Ethical dimensions of climate change include historical responsibility of developed nations, intergenerational justice, and the disproportionate impact on poor nations (climate justice). The precautionary principle advises action even in the face of scientific uncertainty.

5.3 Indian Environmental Ethics

Concepts from Indian traditions: Ahimsa (non‑violence), reverence for nature (trees, rivers). The Chipko movement and Narmada Bachao Andolan illustrate grassroots environmental activism rooted in ethical concerns.

📌 Unit 6: Media Ethics

▼

6.1 Freedom of the Press and Responsibility

Media plays a crucial role in democracy as the "fourth estate." However, with freedom comes responsibility. Ethical journalism requires truthfulness, accuracy, impartiality, accountability, and minimising harm. The Press Council of India provides guidelines for ethical conduct.

6.2 Key Issues

  • Privacy vs. Public Interest: How much private information can be disclosed?
  • Sensationalism and Trial by Media: Reporting that prejudices judicial proceedings.
  • Fake News and Misinformation: Ethical duty to verify facts before publication.
  • Conflict of Interest: Paid news, advertising disguised as content.

6.3 Digital Media Ethics

New challenges: online harassment, echo chambers, algorithmic bias, deepfakes, and the ethics of content moderation. The principle of net neutrality and its moral implications.

📌 Unit 7: Business Ethics

▼

7.1 Concept and Importance

Business ethics applies moral principles to business conduct. It goes beyond legal compliance to include fairness, honesty, and respect for stakeholders. Ethical businesses build trust, attract talent, and sustain long‑term profitability. Corporate scandals (Enron, Satyam) highlight the consequences of unethical behaviour.

7.2 Stakeholder Theory

Ed Freeman argued that businesses should consider the interests of all stakeholders—employees, customers, suppliers, community, environment—not just shareholders. This contrasts with the shareholder primacy model (Milton Friedman).

7.3 Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

CSR refers to a company's voluntary commitment to social and environmental goals. In India, Section 135 of the Companies Act 2013 mandates CSR spending of 2% of average net profits for qualifying companies. Ethical CSR goes beyond compliance, integrating social good into core business strategy.

7.4 Ethical Issues in Business

  • Insider trading, bribery, corruption.
  • Discrimination and harassment.
  • False advertising, deceptive packaging.
  • Labour exploitation, child labour, sweatshops.
  • Environmental pollution and greenwashing.

📌 Unit 8: Computer Ethics and Cyber Ethics

▼

8.1 Defining Computer Ethics

Computer ethics examines the moral issues related to the use of computer technology, including software piracy, hacking, privacy, and intellectual property. James Moor argued that computers create policy vacuums because they enable new possibilities not covered by existing ethical norms.

8.2 Key Issues

  • Privacy: Data collection, surveillance, biometric databases (Aadhaar debate).
  • Intellectual Property: Software piracy, open‑source movement, digital rights management.
  • Cybercrime: Hacking, phishing, identity theft, ransomware.
  • AI and Algorithms: Bias, accountability, autonomous weapons, job displacement.

8.3 Professional Codes

ACM Code of Ethics, IEEE Code of Conduct, and Indian IT Act 2000 provide legal and ethical frameworks. The Ten Commandments of Computer Ethics by the Computer Ethics Institute: e.g., "Thou shalt not use a computer to harm other people."

📌 Unit 9: Ethics in Public Life

▼

9.1 Importance of Public Ethics

Public officials and politicians hold positions of trust; ethical governance requires integrity, transparency, accountability, and impartiality. Corruption erodes public confidence and harms the most vulnerable. International frameworks like the UN Convention against Corruption and India's Right to Information Act promote ethical governance.

9.2 Whistle‑blowing

Whistle‑blowing is the disclosure of illegal, unethical, or harmful practices within an organisation. Ethical dilemmas: loyalty vs. public interest. Conditions for justified whistle‑blowing (De George): serious harm, exhausted internal channels, clear evidence, chance of success.

9.3 Code of Conduct for Public Servants

In India, the Central Civil Services (Conduct) Rules prescribe ethical behaviour. Key values: devotion to duty, honesty, political neutrality, and avoidance of conflict of interest.

📌 Unit 10: Professional Ethics

▼

10.1 Nature of Professions

A profession is a vocation requiring specialised knowledge, self‑regulation, and a commitment to public service beyond profit. Professionals are granted autonomy in exchange for adhering to ethical codes. Examples: medicine, law, engineering, teaching.

10.2 Professional Codes of Ethics

Codes provide guidance, maintain standards, and protect the public. Medical ethics: Hippocratic Oath, MCI Code. Legal ethics: Bar Council of India Rules. Engineering ethics: IEEE Code. Common themes: competence, integrity, confidentiality, conflict of interest disclosure, and continuing education.

10.3 Ethical Dilemmas in Professional Life

Whistle‑blowing, dual loyalty (to employer vs. public), professional negligence, discrimination. Solving dilemmas through ethical decision‑making models: identify facts, stakeholders, principles, evaluate options, and select the best course.

📝 Solved Previous Year Questions (PYQs) – BPYI 101

▼

Short Answer Questions

Q1. Define applied ethics. 2 MARKS

Answer: Applied ethics is the branch of ethics that deals with the practical application of moral principles to specific real‑world issues such as euthanasia, climate change, media freedom, and business conduct. It bridges normative theories and everyday moral dilemmas.

Q2. What are the four principles of bioethics? 5 MARKS

Answer: Beauchamp & Childress's four principles are: Autonomy (respecting patient's right to make decisions), Beneficence (obligation to do good), Non‑maleficence (avoiding harm), and Justice (fair distribution of healthcare resources). These guide clinical and research ethics.

Q3. Differentiate between act and rule utilitarianism. 5 MARKS

Answer: Act utilitarianism evaluates each individual action by its consequences (greatest happiness). Rule utilitarianism follows general rules that, when universally adopted, produce the greatest good. Act utilitarianism may justify lying in a specific case; rule utilitarianism would forbid lying because the rule against lying maximises overall trust and happiness.

Long Answer Questions

Q4. Discuss the ethical issues surrounding euthanasia with reference to Indian court judgements. 10 MARKS

Answer: Euthanasia raises conflicts between autonomy, sanctity of life, and the medical duty to preserve life. In India, the Supreme Court in Common Cause vs. Union of India (2018) legalised passive euthanasia and advance medical directives (living wills) under strict safeguards. The Aruna Shanbaug case (2011) earlier allowed passive euthanasia for the first time. Pro‑arguments include individual autonomy and relief from suffering; opponents cite slippery slope and devaluation of life. The judgement balances individual rights with the state's interest in preserving life.

Q5. Explain the stakeholder theory and contrast it with the shareholder primacy model. 10 MARKS

Answer: Freeman's stakeholder theory argues that businesses have ethical obligations to all groups affected by their actions—employees, customers, suppliers, community, and environment—not just shareholders. The shareholder primacy model (Friedman) holds that the only social responsibility of business is to increase profits within the law. Stakeholder theory promotes long‑term sustainability and corporate social responsibility, while shareholder primacy prioritises short‑term financial returns. Real‑world examples include CSR‑driven companies vs. profit‑maximising firms with poor labour practices.
