📌 Unit 1: Communication – An Overview

▼

1.1 Definition & Meaning

Communication is the process of exchanging information, ideas, thoughts, and feelings between individuals through a common system of symbols, signs, or behaviour. The word derives from the Latin communicare, meaning "to share". Effective communication occurs when the intended message is received and understood.

Mnemonic: "S-M-C-R-F" — Elements of Communication: Sender, Message, Channel, Receiver, Feedback

1.2 Process of Communication

  1. Sender: Initiates the message.
  2. Encoding: Converting thoughts into symbols/words.
  3. Message: The information to be transmitted.
  4. Channel: Medium used (speech, writing, digital).
  5. Receiver: Person for whom the message is intended.
  6. Decoding: Interpreting the message.
  7. Feedback: Response from receiver confirming understanding.

1.3 Functions of Communication

  • Information sharing: Conveying facts, data.
  • Persuasion: Influencing attitudes or behaviour.
  • Emotional expression: Sharing feelings.
  • Social interaction: Building relationships.
  • Decision-making: Aiding in problem-solving.

Exam Tip: Always illustrate the communication process with a diagram and label Sender, Encoding, Message, Channel, Decoding, Receiver, Feedback, Noise.

📌 Unit 2: Types of Communication

▼

2.1 Based on Expression

Verbal: Oral (face-to-face, telephone) and written (letters, emails). Non-verbal: Body language, gestures, facial expressions, posture, eye contact. Visual: Graphs, charts, signs.

2.2 Based on Context

  • Intrapersonal: Self-talk, internal dialogue.
  • Interpersonal: Between two or more people.
  • Group: Within a small team or committee.
  • Mass: Large audiences via media.
  • Formal: Official channels, structured.
  • Informal: Grapevine, casual.

Mnemonic: "VIN-GIM" — Types of Communication: Verbal, Interpersonal, Non-verbal, Group, Intrapersonal, Mass

📌 Unit 3: Models of Communication

▼

3.1 Linear Models

Aristotle's Model: Speaker → Speech → Audience (one-way, persuasive). Lasswell's Model (1948): Who → Says What → Channel → To Whom → Effect.

3.2 Shannon-Weaver Model (1949)

Introduced noise as a barrier. Information source → Transmitter → Channel → Receiver → Destination. Emphasizes technical aspect.

3.3 Interactive Models

Osgood & Schramm (1954): Circular model; sender and receiver encode/decode simultaneously. Berlo's SMCR: Source, Message, Channel, Receiver – each influenced by multiple factors (skills, attitudes, knowledge).

Exam Tip: Be prepared to draw and explain the Shannon-Weaver and Berlo's SMCR models. They are frequently asked.

📌 Unit 4: Barriers to Communication

▼

4.1 Types of Barriers

  • Physical: Noise, distance, faulty equipment.
  • Psychological: Emotions, prejudice, selective perception.
  • Linguistic/Semantic: Jargon, ambiguous words, language differences.
  • Cultural: Different norms, values, body language interpretation.
  • Organizational: Hierarchical structure, information overload, poor channels.

4.2 Overcoming Barriers

Use simple language, active listening, empathy, feedback, appropriate channel selection, and cultural sensitivity.

Mnemonic: "PLACE" — Barriers: Physical, Linguistic, Attitudinal (psychological), Cultural, External (organizational)

📌 Unit 5: Mass Communication & Media

▼

5.1 Characteristics of Mass Communication

Large, heterogeneous, anonymous audience; use of technology; rapid transmission; public message; indirect feedback. Functions: Surveillance, correlation, cultural transmission, entertainment (Lasswell & Wright).

5.2 Theories of Media Effects

  • Hypodermic Needle: Direct, powerful effect (now outdated).
  • Two-Step Flow: Opinion leaders mediate media influence.
  • Uses & Gratifications: Audience actively selects media to satisfy needs.
  • Agenda-Setting: Media tells us what to think about.

📌 Unit 6: Print Media

▼

6.1 Types & Characteristics

Newspapers: Daily, regional, national; provide news, analysis. Magazines: Periodical, niche topics, longer articles. Books: In-depth knowledge, permanence. Print media has tangibility, credibility, detailed coverage, but lacks immediacy and interactivity.

6.2 Role in Society

Watchdog of democracy, agenda-setter, educator, platform for public debate. In India, the press is free under Article 19(1)(a) with reasonable restrictions.

📌 Unit 7: Radio

▼

7.1 Characteristics of Radio

Theatre of the mind; portable, affordable, accessible in rural areas; intimate medium; relies on sound (speech, music, effects). Strengths: Wide reach, immediacy, low cost. Limitations: One-way, no visual, fleeting.

7.2 Community Radio & All India Radio

Community radio serves local needs, promotes grassroots participation. AIR (Akashvani) is India's public broadcaster, covering news, education, and entertainment.

📌 Unit 10: Advertising

▼

10.1 Definition & Objectives

Advertising is a paid, non-personal communication by an identified sponsor to promote ideas, goods, or services. Objectives: Inform, persuade, remind, build brand image.

10.2 AIDA Model & Types

AIDA: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. Types: Print, broadcast (TV/radio), outdoor, digital, social media ads.

Mnemonic: "AIDA" — Advertising effectiveness: Attention → Interest → Desire → Action

📝 Solved Previous Year Questions (PYQs) – BEGG 171

▼

📌 Section A – Short Answer Questions

Q1. Define communication. 2 MARKS

Answer: Communication is the process of exchanging information, ideas, and feelings through a common system of symbols. It involves a sender, message, channel, receiver, and feedback. The word derives from Latin communicare meaning "to share".

Q2. What is grapevine communication? 3 MARKS

Answer: Grapevine refers to informal, unofficial communication network within an organization. It spreads rapidly and can carry rumours, but also helps gauge employee sentiment. It is flexible, fast, but can distort messages.

Q3. Explain the AIDA model. 3 MARKS

Answer: AIDA is a communication model used in advertising to describe the stages a consumer goes through: Attention (capture interest), Interest (arouse curiosity), Desire (create want), and Action (prompt purchase).

Q4. Differentiate between skimming and scanning. 3 MARKS

Answer: Skimming is reading quickly to get the gist/main idea; Scanning is reading rapidly to locate specific information (e.g., a date or name). Skimming is used for overview; scanning for precise data.

Q5. What is media convergence? 5 MARKS

Answer: Media convergence is the merging of previously distinct media technologies and platforms due to digitization. For example, a smartphone converges telephone, internet, camera, radio, and television. It leads to cross-platform content, economic integration, and changes in audience behaviour.

📌 Section B – Long Answer Questions

Q6. Discuss the functions of mass communication. 10 MARKS

Answer: According to Lasswell and Wright, mass communication serves four main functions: Surveillance (providing news and information), Correlation (interpretation of events, shaping public opinion), Cultural Transmission (passing on values, norms), and Entertainment (amusement and relaxation). In democratic societies, it also acts as a watchdog and facilitates public debate.

Q7. Compare the Shannon-Weaver and Osgood-Schramm models of communication. 10 MARKS

Answer: Shannon-Weaver (1949) is a linear model emphasizing technical accuracy and noise. It includes sender, encoder, channel, decoder, receiver, and noise. Osgood-Schramm (1954) is a circular model where both parties act as encoder, interpreter, and decoder simultaneously, making communication continuous and two-way. While Shannon-Weaver suits mass communication, Osgood-Schramm fits interpersonal communication better.
