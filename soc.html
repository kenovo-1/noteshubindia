UNIT 1 — Introduction to Social Psychology

Core idea

Social psychology studies how we feel, think, and behave toward others, and how others influence our thoughts, feelings, and behaviour in social contexts.

Definition and scope

  • Definition: scientific study of social behaviour, social thought, and social influence.
  • Nature: scientific because it uses observation, description, measurement, prediction, and replication.
  • Focus: individuals in social situations, not isolated behaviour.
  • Scope: attitudes, social cognition, social influence, pro-social and anti-social behaviour, groups, identity, culture, and leadership.

Historical development

  • Early roots in philosophy and social thought.
  • 1860: Völkerpsychologie and collective mind ideas.
  • 1879: Wundt's psychology laboratory made psychology experimental.
  • 1913: Watson pushed behaviourism and observable behaviour.
  • 1924: Floyd Allport argued for experimental social psychology.
  • 1940s–50s: Lewin and Festinger strengthened scientific social psychology.
  • Late 20th century: social cognition, persuasion, cognitive dissonance, decision making.
  • 21st century: social neuroscience and culture-based research expanded the field.
  • India: early book in 1928; later work on rumours, prejudice, identity, self, culture, and achievement motivation.

Mnemonic

S-B-C = Science, Behaviour, Context. Social psychology is the science of behaviour in context.

Important points

  • Social psychology is not just common sense.
  • It studies actual, imagined, and implied presence of others.
  • Kurt Lewin's formula: behaviour depends on the person and the environment.

Important questions

  • Define social psychology and explain its nature and scope.
  • Trace the historical development of social psychology.
  • Write a note on the development of social psychology in India.

UNIT 2 — Theories of Social Psychology

Links with other disciplines

  • Anthropology: culture, shared meanings, beliefs, norms, practices.
  • Economics: cost, reward, investment, choice, and social exchange.
  • Political science: voting, ideology, persuasion, leadership, political behaviour.
  • Sociology: groups, institutions, and social structure; sociology looks more at the group, social psychology more at the individual in the group.
  • Biological psychology: brain, hormones, nervous system, social neuroscience.
  • Clinical psychology: abnormal behaviour; social psychology helps explain normal social behaviour too.
  • Cognitive psychology: memory, attention, judgment, problem solving, decision making.
  • Developmental psychology: changes across the life span and how social behaviour develops.
  • Personality psychology: individual differences, inner processes, situation-person interaction.

Levels of analysis of social behaviour

  • Intrapersonal: social cognition, values, attitudes, violent behaviour, deviance.
  • Interpersonal: cooperation, conflict, altruism, aggression, prejudice, discrimination.
  • Individual-group: conformity, socialization, norms, role learning, identity.
  • Group-group: intergroup relations, prejudice, conflict, discrimination.

Theoretical approaches

  • Learning theories: behaviour is shaped by rewards, punishments, and observed consequences.
  • Cognitive theories: behaviour depends on how people perceive the situation and process information.

Research methods and ethics

  • Observational method: naturalistic or systematic observation of behaviour.
  • Experimental method: manipulation of variables to test cause and effect.
  • Quasi-experimental method: comparison without full random assignment.
  • Ethics: informed consent, minimal harm, privacy, confidentiality, no unnecessary deception, debriefing, voluntary participation.

Mnemonic

A-B-C-P = Anthropology, Biology, Cognition, Personality for the major links; and L-C = Learning + Cognition for the two main theoretical approaches.

Important questions

  • Explain the relationship between social psychology and other disciplines.
  • Discuss the levels of analysis of social behaviour.
  • Compare learning theories and cognitive theories of social behaviour.
  • Describe research methods and ethical issues in social psychology.

UNIT 3 — Self and Its Processes

Self-concept

  • Self-concept is the person's understanding of who they are.
  • It includes identity, abilities, personality, roles, family membership, culture, and organizational belonging.
  • It changes with situation, interaction, and experience.

Formation of self-concept

  • Looking-glass self: we form our image by imagining how others see us.
  • Social comparison: we evaluate ourselves by comparing with others; reference groups matter.

Self-esteem and self-efficacy

  • Self-esteem: evaluation of one's self-concept.
  • Self-efficacy: belief in one's ability to perform a task in a specific context.

Impression formation, impression management, and self-presentation

  • People try to create and control the image others form of them.
  • Impression management is the broader process of guiding how others see us.
  • Self-presentation includes strategies like ingratiation, intimidation, self-promotion, exemplification, and supplication.
  • Individual differences matter: some people are more concerned with image, status, approval, or competence.

Mnemonic

L-S = Looking-glass + Social comparison for self-concept formation.

I I S E S = Ingratiation, Intimidation, Self-promotion, Exemplification, Supplication.

Important questions

  • Explain self-concept and its formation.
  • Differentiate self-esteem and self-efficacy.
  • Discuss impression formation and self-presentation tactics.

UNIT 4 — Self in Social Context

Culture: meaning and definition

  • Culture is learned, shared, transmitted, and dynamic.
  • It includes values, beliefs, norms, roles, behaviours, and meanings.
  • Different views of culture include descriptive, historical, normative, psychological, structural, and genetic perspectives.

Enculturation and acculturation

  • Enculturation: learning one's own culture through social agents.
  • Acculturation: adapting to another culture.
  • Agents of enculturation: parents and siblings, extended family, peers, education, religion.

Self across cultures

  • Individualist cultures: autonomy, personal goals, independence.
  • Collectivist cultures: interdependence, harmony, group goals.
  • Multicultural identity can work at intrapersonal, interpersonal, and collective levels.

Social behaviour across cultures

  • Group membership, ingroup identification, and ingroup bias vary across cultures.
  • Culture affects attribution style, aggression, person perception, attraction, and relationships.
  • Collectivist cultures generally show stronger ingroup orientation and more sensitivity to social norms.

Mnemonic

P-E-P-E-R = Parents, Extended family, Peers, Education, Religion as enculturation agents.

Important questions

  • Explain enculturation and acculturation.
  • Discuss individualist and collectivist cultures.
  • Describe multicultural identity and cultural influences on behaviour.

UNIT 5 — Social Cognition: Understanding Social Behaviour I

Meaning of social cognition

  • Social cognition is the process of interpreting, analysing, remembering, and using information about the social world.
  • It studies how we register, encode, classify, store, and use social information.

Schema

  • Schemas are cognitive structures that organise social information and expectations.
  • They help with encoding, storage, retrieval, and prediction.
  • Types: person schemas, self schemas, group schemas, role schemas, event schemas.
  • Group schemas are closely linked to stereotypes.

Modes of social thought processing

  • Continuum model: people may move from more automatic to more controlled processing.
  • Automatic processing: fast, effortless, and often unconscious.
  • Controlled processing: slow, effortful, and conscious.

Heuristics

  • Availability: judge by what comes to mind easily.
  • Representativeness: judge by similarity to a prototype.
  • Anchoring and adjustment: start from an initial value and adjust from there.

Sources of errors in social cognition

  • Cognitive-experiential self theory: people often rely on intuitive, experience-based thinking.
  • Paying attention to inconsistent information: unusual or conflicting data distorts judgment.
  • Negativity bias: negative information weighs more strongly than positive information.
  • Planning fallacy: people underestimate time and difficulty.
  • Thinking too much: overanalysis can produce poor judgment.
  • Counterfactual thinking: "what if" thinking after events.
  • Magical thinking: irrational links based on similarity or contagion.

Mnemonic

P-S-G-R-E = Person, Self, Group, Role, Event schemas.

A-R-A = Availability, Representativeness, Anchoring.

Important questions

  • Define social cognition and schema.
  • Describe the types of schema and their impact.
  • Explain heuristics and sources of error in social cognition.

UNIT 6 — Social Cognition: Understanding Social Behaviour II

Person perception and impression formation

  • Person perception is how we interpret others.
  • Impression formation is the process of forming an overall view of another person.
  • Trait centrality: some traits matter more than others in forming the final impression.
  • First impression: early information strongly shapes later judgment.

Theories of attribution

  • Heider's naive psychology: people act like everyday scientists and infer causes of behaviour.
  • Correspondent inference theory: people infer stable traits from behaviour that seems intentional and freely chosen.
  • Covariation model: people use consistency, distinctiveness, and consensus to infer causes.
  • Attribution of success and failure: success and failure are explained through internal or external causes.

Errors and biases in attribution

  • Fundamental attribution error: overemphasising personality and underestimating situation.
  • Actor-observer bias: actors explain their own actions by situation, observers explain by traits.
  • Self-serving bias: success is credited to self, failure to external causes.
  • Ultimate attribution error: negative acts of outgroup members are seen as internal; positive acts as external.

Mnemonic

C-C-C = Consistency, Distinctiveness, Consensus in covariation model.

F-A-S-U = Fundamental, Actor-observer, Self-serving, Ultimate attribution errors.

Important questions

  • Explain person perception and impression formation.
  • Discuss Heider's, correspondent inference, and covariation theories.
  • Explain attribution errors with examples.

UNIT 7 — Attitude and Attitude Change

Meaning and structure of attitude

  • Attitude is a mental and neural readiness formed through experience that guides responses.
  • Three components: cognitive, affective, behavioural.

Types and functions of attitudes

  • Attitudes may be positive, negative, or neutral.
  • They can be based on object, issue, person, or situation.
  • Functions include knowledge, instrumental, ego-defensive, and value-expressive functions.

Attitude formation

  • Mere exposure: repeated contact increases liking.
  • Personal experience: direct experience shapes attitudes strongly.
  • Classical conditioning: association with positive or negative stimuli.
  • Operant conditioning: reinforcement or punishment.
  • Observational learning: learning from models.
  • Genetic factors: biological predispositions also matter.

Attitude change

  • Balance theory: people prefer consistency among related attitudes and relationships.
  • Cognitive dissonance theory: inconsistency creates discomfort, so people change beliefs or behaviour.
  • Forced compliance, decision making, and effort justification are key applications.
  • Persuasion: attitude change through communication; dual-process approaches explain how people process persuasive messages.

Attitude and behaviour

  • Specificity, accessibility, self-awareness, certainty, and strength influence whether attitudes predict behaviour.

Stereotype, prejudice, discrimination

  • Stereotype: general belief about a group.
  • Prejudice: negative attitude toward a group.
  • Discrimination: unfair behaviour toward a group.
  • Reduction methods: social learning, intergroup contact, recategorisation, guilt, and stereotype negation.
  • Social distance shows willingness to interact closely or keep distance from groups.
  • Attitude can be measured using questionnaires and indirect methods.

Mnemonic

C-A-B = Cognitive, Affective, Behavioural components of attitude.

M-P-C-O-G = Mere exposure, Personal experience, Classical conditioning, Operant conditioning, Genetics for attitude formation.

Important questions

  • Define attitude and explain its components.
  • Discuss attitude formation and attitude change.
  • Explain stereotype, prejudice, and discrimination.
  • Describe the relationship between attitude and behaviour.

UNIT 8 — Social Influence

Three forms of social influence

  • Conformity: change in behaviour or belief to match the majority or social norm.
  • Compliance: agreeing to a direct request from another person.
  • Obedience: following direct orders from an authority.

Conformity

  • Asch is the classic researcher on conformity.
  • Reasons include desire to be liked, desire to be right, and social acceptance.
  • Factors affecting conformity include group size, unanimity, task importance, and culture.
  • Resistance to conformity depends on individuality, personal control, and norms that support dissent.
  • Minority influence works when the minority is consistent, flexible, and socially relevant.

Compliance

  • Professionals like advertisers and politicians use compliance techniques.
  • Foot-in-the-door: start small, then ask bigger.
  • Lowball: make offer attractive first, then reduce benefits.
  • Door-in-the-face: start with a large request, then move to a smaller one.
  • That's-not-all: add bonus before the target decides.
  • Playing hard to get: create scarcity and difficulty.
  • Deadline technique: limited time creates pressure.

Obedience

  • Milgram's experiment showed that ordinary people can obey destructive orders.
  • Reasons include authority symbols, diffusion of responsibility, escalation of orders, and low time for reflection.
  • Resistance requires questioning authority, remembering personal responsibility, and exposure to disobedient models.

Mnemonic

C-C-O = Conformity, Compliance, Obedience.

F-L-D-T-P-D = Foot-in-the-door, Lowball, Door-in-the-face, That's-not-all, Playing hard to get, Deadline.

Important questions

  • Explain conformity and factors affecting it.
  • Describe compliance techniques with examples.
  • Discuss Milgram's obedience experiment.
  • Explain resistance to conformity and obedience.

UNIT 9 — Aggression and Social Influence

Meaning and forms of aggression

  • Aggression is behaviour aimed at causing physical or psychological harm.
  • Forms include physical aggression, verbal aggression, instrumental aggression, hostile aggression, and bullying-related harm.

Theoretical approaches

  • Biological approaches: aggression linked to innate tendencies and bodily processes.
  • Drive approaches: aggression comes from a harmful drive.
  • Social learning approaches: aggression is learned by direct or vicarious experience.
  • Frustration-aggression hypothesis: frustration increases aggression.
  • Excitation transfer theory: arousal carries over to later situations and can intensify aggression.
  • General aggression model: integrates social, cognitive, developmental, and biological factors.

Causes of aggression

  • Personal: frustration, provocation, personality, biochemical influences, displaced aggression, age, gender.
  • Interpersonal/social/cultural: mass media, social rejection, need fulfilment, influence.
  • Situational/environmental: weapon effect, environmental discomfort.

Reducing aggression

  • Social learning: punishment and modelling.
  • Catharsis.
  • Cognitive strategies.

Bullying behaviour

  • Repeated harm against a weaker target.
  • Motives: power and group status.
  • Reduction: environmental sensitisation, victim education, awareness.

Mnemonic

B-D-S-F-E-G = Biological, Drive, Social learning, Frustration, Excitation, General aggression model.

P-I-S = Personal, Interpersonal/social, Situational causes of aggression.

Important questions

  • Define aggression and discuss its forms.
  • Explain the major theories of aggression.
  • Discuss causes and reduction of aggression.
  • Write a note on bullying behaviour and its control.

UNIT 10 — Interpersonal Attraction and Pro-Social Behaviour

Interpersonal attraction

  • Interpersonal attraction is a prolonged relationship based on liking.
  • Internal factors: need to affiliate, positive affect.
  • External factors: proximity, mere exposure, physical attractiveness.
  • Interpersonal factors: similarity and reciprocity.

Pro-social behaviour

  • Pro-social behaviour benefits others or society even when the helper gets no immediate benefit.
  • Motives include empathy-altruism, negative-state relief, empathic joy, competitive altruism, and kin selection.

Bystander effect

  • Latané and Darley's five-stage model explains why people fail to help in emergencies.
  • Help depends on noticing the event, interpreting it as an emergency, taking responsibility, knowing how to help, and deciding to act.

Factors affecting pro-social behaviour

  • Similarity with the victim.
  • Attribution about the victim's suffering.
  • Pro-social modelling.
  • Gratitude and positive reinforcement.
  • Interpersonal connection and social belonging.

Mnemonic

N-P-P-S-R = Need to affiliate, Proximity, Physical attractiveness, Similarity, Reciprocity.

E-N-E-C-K = Empathy-altruism, Negative-state relief, Empathic joy, Competitive altruism, Kin selection.

Important questions

  • Explain interpersonal attraction and its factors.
  • Define pro-social behaviour and discuss its motives.
  • Describe the bystander effect and helping behaviour.
  • Discuss factors that increase pro-social behaviour.

UNIT 11 — Group Processes

Groups: definition and concept

  • A group is a collection of people who see themselves as a bonded unit.
  • Members have common motives, interact, depend on one another, and influence each other.
  • Group is different from a crowd and different from a team.

Types of groups

  • Common-bond and common-identity groups.
  • Primary and secondary groups.
  • Formal and informal groups.
  • High and low entitativity depending on how coherent the group is seen.

Components of group

  • Status: rank or position.
  • Roles: expected behaviour in the group.
  • Norms: implicit rules.
  • Cohesiveness: force that keeps a group together.

Stages of group formation

  • Forming.
  • Storming.
  • Norming.
  • Performing.
  • Adjourning, when the group ends.

Group processes

  • Social facilitation: others' presence changes performance.
  • Social loafing: less effort when responsibility is shared.
  • Deindividuation: loss of self-awareness in a group context.

Group decision making

  • Group polarisation: group moves toward more extreme positions.
  • Groupthink: highly cohesive groups suppress dissent and overestimate correctness.

Cooperation, competition, conflict

  • Cooperation = working together toward a shared goal.
  • Competition = one member's gain depends on another's loss.
  • Conflict = perceived incompatibility of interests.
  • Communication shapes group functioning and conflict resolution.

Communication

  • Types, styles, and barriers to communication are part of group life.

Mnemonic

F-S-N-P = Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing.

S-L-D = Social facilitation, Social loafing, Deindividuation.

Important questions

  • Define a group and differentiate it from a crowd and a team.
  • Explain group formation stages.
  • Discuss social facilitation, social loafing, and groupthink.
  • Explain cooperation, competition, conflict, and communication in groups.

UNIT 12 — Leader and Leadership

Concept and role of leader

  • A leader has the greatest impact on group beliefs and actions.
  • Leaders initiate action, make decisions, solve problems, resolve conflicts, inspire members, and guide the group.
  • Leadership may be formal or informal.

Characteristics of a leader

  • Integrity and honesty.
  • Communication.
  • Self-esteem and confidence.
  • Empathy.
  • Maturity.
  • Physical presence and influence.

Styles of leadership

  • Autocratic: leader controls decisions.
  • Participative: group members share decisions.
  • Consultative: leader takes input seriously before deciding.
  • Bureaucratic: leadership through rules and procedures.
  • Narcissistic: leader is self-focused and self-serving.
  • Laissez-faire: minimal direct control.

Social categorization

  • People classify others by categories such as gender, race, and social group.
  • These categories shape perception, expectations, and leadership judgments.

Role of leader in conflict resolution

  • Leaders may compete, accommodate, avoid, collaborate, or compromise depending on the situation.
  • Thomas-Kilmann's model includes five conflict styles: competing, accommodating, avoiding, collaborating, and compromising.

Mnemonic

I-C-S-E-M-P = Integrity, Communication, Self-esteem, Empathy, Maturity, Presence.

A-P-C-B-N-L = Autocratic, Participative, Consultative, Bureaucratic, Narcissistic, Laissez-faire.

C-A-A-C-C = Competing, Accommodating, Avoiding, Collaborating, Compromising.

Important questions

  • Define leader and explain the role of leadership.
  • Describe the characteristics and styles of leadership.
  • Write a short note on social categorization.
  • Explain Thomas-Kilmann's conflict resolution styles.
