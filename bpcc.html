Unit 1 — Origin of Psychology

Definition, evolution, behaviour, and science

Meaning of psychology

Psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes. The book moves through four stages of definition: soul, mind, consciousness, and behaviour. The change happened because psychology gradually became more scientific and less philosophical.

The early “science of soul” definition was rejected because soul is metaphysical. The “science of mind” definition was rejected because mind cannot be directly observed. The “science of consciousness” definition was rejected because consciousness is subjective and excludes unconscious and animal behaviour. The modern view focuses on behaviour and cognitive processes.

Behaviour characteristics

  • Behaviour is influenced by biological, cultural, social, environmental, past-experience, motivational, emotional, and cognitive factors.
  • Behaviour varies in complexity from reflexes to skilled and highly complex actions.
  • Behaviour is purposeful and goal-directed.
  • Behaviour can change through experience and learning.
  • Behaviour also shows stability and integration.

Historical development

Psychology became an independent discipline in 1879 when Wilhelm Wundt established the first laboratory at Leipzig. Wundt studied the structure of conscious experience. Watson later pushed behaviourism and insisted on observable behaviour. Woodworth’s sentence about psychology losing its soul, mind, and consciousness summarises this shift.

Psychology as a science

  • Empirical observation: science depends on observation and experiment, not opinion.
  • Systematic approach: data are collected, analysed, and interpreted in a structured way.
  • Measurement: events are assigned numbers according to rules.
  • Operational definitions: concepts are defined by measurable behaviour.
  • Replication: findings can be repeated and checked.
MnemonicS-M-B = Soul, Mind, Behaviour in the evolution of definition.

Unit 2 — Nature and Scope of Psychology

Other sciences, branches, subfields, and psychology in India

Relation to other sciences

Psychology does not stand alone. It is closely related to biology, sociology, medicine, anthropology, economics, political science, and other disciplines. The book stresses transactionalism: serious human problems often require multiple sciences to work together.

Basic and applied branches

Basic psychology aims to understand general principles and mechanisms. Applied psychology uses those principles to solve practical problems in work, education, health, law, and community life.

Subfields

  • Biopsychology: biological bases of behaviour, brain, genes, hormones.
  • Cognitive psychology: memory, thinking, problem solving, reasoning, language.
  • Comparative psychology: comparison of species, especially animals.
  • Cultural psychology: behaviour across culture and subculture.
  • Experimental psychology: perception, learning, motivation, emotion using controlled experiments.
  • Gender psychology: roles and influences of gender throughout life.
  • Learning psychology: how and why learning occurs.
  • Personality psychology: traits and dynamics, assessment and development.
  • Physiological psychology: biochemical and nervous system bases of behaviour.
  • Sensation and perception psychology: sense organs and perceptual mechanisms.
  • Social psychology: attitudes, conformity, persuasion, prejudice, friendship, aggression, helping.
  • Clinical psychology: diagnosis and treatment of severe psychological disorders.
  • Community psychology: prevention, education, consultation, community mental health.
  • Consumer psychology: packaging, advertising, marketing, consumer behaviour.
  • Counselling psychology: personal, career, and adjustment problems.
  • Educational psychology: classroom dynamics, teaching styles, curriculum, learning problems.
  • Ergonomics: work environment, products, comfort, and performance.
  • Industrial/Organisational psychology: selection, appraisal, motivation, leadership, work conflict.
  • Medical psychology: emotional impact of illness and compliance with treatment.
  • Forensic psychology: psychology and law, assessment, expert testimony.
  • Military psychology: behaviour of military forces, stress, readiness, enemy behaviour.
  • Environmental psychology: human-environment interaction.
  • Sports psychology: performance, motivation, teamwork, exercise, wellbeing.

Psychology in India

Indian psychology has ancient roots in the Vedas, Yoga Sutras, Bhagavad Gita, and epic literature. In modern academic form, psychology began in Calcutta University in 1916. The Indian Psychological Association was established in 1924 and the Indian Journal of Psychology started in 1925. After independence, applied psychology and indigenous studies grew strongly.

MnemonicB-C-C-G-L-P-S = Biopsychology, Cognitive, Comparative, Gender, Learning, Personality, Social as a quick branch recall chain.

Unit 3 — Perception: Meaning, Laws and Perceptual Constancies

Stages of perception, attention, Gestalt principles, and constancies

Meaning and stages

Perception is the process of selecting, organising, and interpreting sensory information. The three stages are selection, organisation, and interpretation. The first stage filters stimuli, the second arranges them into meaningful patterns, and the third assigns meaning based on experience and expectation.

Theoretical approaches

  • Bottom-up processing: starts with raw sensory data.
  • Top-down processing: starts with prior knowledge and expectations.

Sensation

Sensation is the basic conversion of physical stimulation into neural activity. Transduction turns light, sound, or pressure into signals the brain can process.

Attention

  • Selective attention: focus on one stimulus while ignoring others.
  • Sustained attention: attention maintained over time.

Gestalt principles

  • Figure-ground: separating the object of focus from background.
  • Proximity: close items are grouped together.
  • Similarity: similar stimuli are grouped together.
  • Good continuation: lines are seen as continuing smoothly.
  • Closure: incomplete figures are mentally completed.
  • Common region/common fate: items moving together or within the same region are grouped.
  • Prägnanz: simplest and most stable organisation is preferred.

Perceptual constancy

  • Size constancy: object remains same-sized in perception.
  • Shape constancy: object keeps same shape despite angle change.
  • Colour/Brightness constancy: colour and brightness remain stable under changing light.

Factors affecting perception

  • Motivation or need
  • Expectation
  • Emotions
  • Stimulus characteristics
  • Experience
  • Culture
MnemonicS-O-I = Selection, Organisation, Interpretation.

Unit 4 — Perception: Types and Errors of Perception

Depth, distance, movement, size perception, and illusions

Depth and distance perception

Depth perception gives us the ability to judge distance and see the world in three dimensions. The book divides depth cues into monocular and binocular cues.

Monocular cues

  • Relative size: smaller retinal image usually means greater distance.
  • Texture gradient: texture becomes less detailed as distance increases.
  • Aerial perspective: distant objects look hazier.
  • Linear perspective: parallel lines seem to meet far away.
  • Interposition: closer objects block distant ones.
  • Accommodation: lens changes help judge distance.

Binocular cues

  • Retinal disparity: two eyes see slightly different images.
  • Convergence: inward eye movement gives depth information.

Movement perception

  • Retinal motion: moving object changes its retinal image.
  • Motion after-effect: after staring at motion, stationary objects seem to move.
  • Induced movement: stationary object appears to move because surrounding field moves.
  • Stroboscopic motion: rapidly shown still images appear to move.

Size perception

The book explains how size perception depends on distance cues and familiar size. The size-distance invariance idea says perceived size is related to perceived distance.

Errors in perception

  • Illusion: wrong perception of a real stimulus.
  • Hallucination: perceiving something that is not there.

Common visual illusions

  • Müller-Lyer illusion
  • Ponzo illusion
  • Ebbinghaus illusion
  • Ames room illusion
  • Moon illusion
MnemonicR-T-A-L-I-A = Relative size, Texture, Aerial perspective, Linear perspective, Interposition, Accommodation.

Unit 5 — Thinking and Language

Thinking, creativity, problem solving, decision making, and language

Thinking

Thinking is a higher mental process done with symbols of language. It is goal-directed and not random. The unit distinguishes perceptual/concrete thinking, conceptual/abstract thinking, reflective thinking, creative thinking, convergent thinking, divergent thinking, and non-directed thinking.

Building blocks of thought

  • Mental imagery: creating pictures in the mind.
  • Concept: a mental category with common properties.
  • Prototype: best or most typical example of a concept.
  • Proposition: a statement about relations between concepts.

Creativity

Creativity is the production of original and useful ideas. Guilford’s distinction between convergent and divergent thinking is central. Wallas’s four stages are preparation, incubation, illumination, and verification.

Problem solving

Problem solving is the process of overcoming obstacles to reach a goal. Sternberg’s stages include identifying the problem, defining it, constructing a strategy, organising information, allocating resources, monitoring progress, and evaluating the result.

Barriers to problem solving

  • Mental set
  • Functional fixedness
  • Incomplete representation
  • Lack of expertise

Decision making

Decision making means choosing among alternatives. Heuristics speed up decisions but can create errors.

  • Representativeness heuristic: similarity to a prototype.
  • Availability heuristic: easy recall feels more likely.
  • Anchoring: first information becomes the base.

Biases

  • Overconfidence bias
  • Hindsight bias
  • Framing effect
  • Confirmation bias

Language

Language is a symbolic system with grammar. Its components are phoneme, morpheme, syntax, semantics, and pragmatics. The unit also covers language development in children and the Sapir-Whorf / linguistic relativity debate.

MnemonicP-I-V-V = Preparation, Incubation, Illumination, Verification.

Unit 6 — Learning

Definition, classical conditioning, operant conditioning, observation, and learning strategies

Nature and scope of learning

Learning is a relatively permanent change in behaviour or knowledge due to experience. It helps with adaptation and survival. The book covers motor, verbal, concept, and discrimination learning.

Classical conditioning

Pavlov showed that a neutral stimulus, when paired repeatedly with an unconditioned stimulus, becomes a conditioned stimulus that elicits a conditioned response. Extinction, spontaneous recovery, generalisation, and discrimination are all important principles. Little Albert is the famous human example used in the book, and learned helplessness is also discussed.

Operant conditioning

Skinner’s operant conditioning is learning through consequences. Reinforcement increases behaviour; punishment decreases it. Positive and negative reinforcement, positive and negative punishment, schedules of reinforcement, shaping, chaining, and the Premack principle are all included.

ScheduleMeaningExample
Fixed ratioReward after a fixed number of responsesFactory workers paid per product
Variable ratioReward after an unpredictable number of responsesSlot machines
Fixed intervalReward after a fixed timeMonthly salary
Variable intervalReward after an unpredictable timeChecking voice mail

Observational learning

Bandura showed that people learn by watching models. The Bobo doll experiment demonstrated that children imitate observed aggression. Attention, retention, reproduction, and motivation are the key processes.

Cognitive learning

Tolman’s latent learning and Köhler’s insight learning show that learning can happen without immediate reinforcement and can occur suddenly through understanding.

Learning strategies

  • Mental imagery: using mental pictures.
  • Rehearsal: repeating information; elaborative rehearsal is stronger than maintenance rehearsal.
  • Organisation: structuring material into meaningful groups.
  • Chunking: grouping units into larger chunks.
  • First-letter technique: making a word from initials.
MnemonicUCS-UCR-CS-CR = basic classical conditioning chain; ARRM = Attention, Retention, Reproduction, Motivation.

  • Explain learning strategies such as imagery, rehearsal, and organisation.

Unit 7 — Memory

Nature of memory, types, models, forgetting, and mnemonics

Nature of memory

Memory is the system that encodes, stores, and retrieves information. The textbook divides memory into declarative/explicit memory, non-declarative/implicit memory, and sensory memory.

Types of memory

  • Working memory: active use of information for immediate tasks.
  • Episodic memory: personal events and life experiences.
  • Semantic memory: facts, meanings, and concepts.
  • Priming: prior exposure influences later response.
  • Conditioning: learned associations.
  • Motor/procedural memory: skills and habits.

Models of memory

The traditional model presents sensory memory, short-term memory, and long-term memory. The level-of-processing model focuses on depth of encoding. The working memory model explains active manipulation through the central executive, phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, and episodic buffer.

ModelCore point
Traditional / multi-storeSensory → STM → LTM
Levels of processingDeep processing creates stronger memory
Working memoryActive system that stores and manipulates information

Forgetting

Ebbinghaus showed a forgetting curve: forgetting is fastest immediately after learning and slows down later. The unit explains interference theory, trace decay, and cue-dependent forgetting.

  • Proactive interference: old learning disrupts new learning.
  • Retroactive interference: new learning disrupts old learning.

Memory enhancement strategies

  • Method of loci: place items in familiar locations.
  • Keyword method: link new words with sound-alike familiar words.
  • Chunking: grouping information meaningfully.
  • First-letter technique: using initials to form a word.

Applications of memory

  • Autobiographical memory: personal past events.
  • False memory: memory for an event that did not occur.
  • Flashbulb memory: vivid memory of emotionally charged events.
MnemonicE-S-R = Encoding, Storage, Retrieval.

Unit 8 — Motivation

Concept, types, and theories of motivation

Concept and nature

Motivation is the internal force that directs, activates, and sustains behaviour toward a goal. It includes need, drive, incentive, and goal. Intrinsic motivation comes from interest and enjoyment, while extrinsic motivation comes from rewards or pressure outside the activity.

Types of motivation

  • Intrinsic motivation
  • Extrinsic motivation
  • Primary drives: hunger, thirst, sleep, sex
  • Secondary drives: achievement, power, affiliation

Theories of motivation

  • Drive reduction: a need creates drive which pushes behaviour.
  • Optimal arousal: performance is best at moderate arousal.
  • Incentive theories: external goals pull behaviour.
  • Maslow: physiological, safety, love/belongingness, esteem, self-actualisation.
  • Alderfer ERG: existence, relatedness, growth.
  • Herzberg: hygiene factors and motivators.
  • McClelland: achievement, affiliation, power.

Motivational concepts

The book also stresses that motivation helps explain individual differences, hidden mental states, and responsibility for action.

MnemonicP-S-L-E-S = Physiological, Safety, Love, Esteem, Self-actualisation.

Unit 9 — Emotions

Emotion, components, theories, and measurement

Meaning of emotion

Emotion is a complex psychological state involving subjective experience, physiological arousal, cognitive appraisal, and behavioural expression. Basic emotions are anger, disgust, happiness, fear, sadness, and surprise. The unit also discusses emotions versus mood and feelings.

Components of emotional process

  • Cognitive appraisal
  • Subjective feeling
  • Physiological arousal
  • Emotional expression
  • Action tendencies
  • Emotional regulation

Theories of emotion

  • James-Lange: arousal comes first, then emotion.
  • Cannon-Bard: arousal and emotion occur simultaneously.
  • Schachter-Singer: arousal plus cognitive label produces emotion.
  • Opponent process: each emotion is followed by an opposite reaction.
  • Cognitive appraisal: emotion depends on how a situation is evaluated.

Measurement of emotions

  • Non-verbal instruments: facial expression, body language, voice.
  • Physiological measures: polygraph and related indicators.
  • Self-report questionnaires: PANAS-X and similar scales.

Emotion regulation and display rules are important because emotions are not always expressed directly. Culture and social context affect how and when emotions are shown.

MnemonicJ-C-S-O-C = James-Lange, Cannon-Bard, Schachter-Singer, Opponent, Cognitive appraisal.
Important exam questions

  • Describe how emotions are measured.
