📌 Unit 1: Introduction to Psychology

▼

1.1 What is Psychology?

Psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes. The term originates from the Greek words psyche (meaning soul, mind, or breath) and logos (meaning study or discourse). Initially psychology was defined as the "science of soul", then as the "science of mind", and later as the "science of consciousness". The modern definition, championed by psychologists like William James and Wilhelm Wundt, encompasses both observable behaviour (actions, responses) and internal mental processes such as thinking, memory, perception, and emotions.

According to the American Psychological Association (APA), psychology is the study of the mind and behaviour, spanning all aspects of the human experience – from the functions of the brain to the actions of nations, from child development to care for the aged. It is both a natural science (studying biological bases) and a social science (studying human interaction).

Mnemonic: "ABC" — Core domains of psychology:

Affect (feelings, emotions) · Behaviour (actions, responses) · Cognition (thoughts, mental processes)

1.2 Goals of Psychology

Psychology aims to achieve four primary goals:

  1. Description: Observing and describing behaviour as accurately as possible. Example: measuring heart rate during a stressful task, or documenting the symptoms of depression.
  2. Explanation: Understanding why a behaviour occurs by identifying its causes. Theories such as the fight‑or‑flight response explain the physiological basis of stress. For example, why do people conform to group pressure?
  3. Prediction: Forecasting future behaviour based on observed patterns. For instance, predicting academic performance from study habits, or predicting which individuals are at risk for anxiety disorders.
  4. Control/Modification: Applying psychological principles to change or influence behaviour for the better, e.g., using cognitive‑behavioural therapy to reduce anxiety, or using reinforcement to improve classroom behaviour.

Exam Tip: Always mention the four goals in order. A common short‑answer question asks, "State the goals of psychology with an example for each." Make sure you can provide a unique example for every goal.

1.3 Psychology as a Science

Psychology follows the scientific method: it formulates hypotheses, collects empirical data through observation and experimentation, analyses results using statistical methods, and draws conclusions. Unlike pseudosciences (astrology, phrenology), psychology relies on controlled studies, replication, and peer review. Important characteristics include objectivity (free from bias), verifiability (can be tested), and systematic inquiry (follows a structured approach).

Key methods include experiments (manipulating variables), surveys (collecting self‑report data), naturalistic observation, and case studies. The use of inferential statistics allows psychologists to determine whether results are significant or due to chance.

1.4 Historical Evolution of Psychology

From ancient Greek philosophers like Plato (nativism – knowledge is innate) and Aristotle (empiricism – knowledge comes from experience), the roots of psychology run deep. The first experimental psychology laboratory was established by Wilhelm Wundt in Leipzig, Germany, in 1879 – widely regarded as the birth of modern scientific psychology. Wundt used introspection to study conscious experience.

In India, the first psychology laboratory was set up at the University of Calcutta in 1915 by N.N. Sengupta. The Indian Psychological Association was founded in 1924. Today, psychology is taught across hundreds of universities in India and applied in clinical, educational, organisational, and forensic settings.

1.5 Key Perspectives in Modern Psychology

  • Biological perspective: Focuses on the brain, nervous system, genetics, and hormones.
  • Behavioural perspective: Emphasises observable behaviour and environmental influences.
  • Cognitive perspective: Studies mental processes like memory, thinking, problem‑solving.
  • Psychodynamic perspective: Focuses on unconscious drives and childhood experiences.
  • Humanistic perspective: Emphasises free will, personal growth, and self‑actualization.
  • Sociocultural perspective: Examines how culture and social context shape behaviour.

📌 Unit 2: Branches of Psychology

▼

2.1 Pure vs. Applied Branches

Psychology is broadly divided into pure/basic branches that focus on developing theories and principles (e.g., cognitive psychology, developmental psychology), and applied branches that solve real‑world problems (e.g., clinical psychology, industrial/organisational psychology). Both are interdependent – applied work often tests theories developed by basic research.

2.2 Major Branches of Psychology

  • Clinical Psychology: Diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of mental disorders. Clinical psychologists use psychotherapy (CBT, psychoanalysis), conduct psychological assessments (MMPI, Rorschach), and work in hospitals, clinics, or private practice. They deal with depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, personality disorders, etc.
  • Counselling Psychology: Helps people with adjustment issues, career choices, relationship problems – generally less severe than clinical cases. Focuses on wellness and personal growth.
  • Cognitive Psychology: Studies mental processes: perception, attention, memory, language, problem‑solving, decision‑making. Key areas include cognitive biases, information processing, and artificial intelligence comparisons.
  • Developmental Psychology: Examines physical, cognitive, social, and emotional changes across the entire lifespan – from prenatal development to old age. Piaget, Vygotsky, Erikson, and Kohlberg are key theorists.
  • Social Psychology: How individuals' thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are influenced by the actual, imagined, or implied presence of others. Topics: conformity (Asch), obedience (Milgram), attitudes, prejudice, group dynamics, attraction.
  • Industrial/Organisational (I/O) Psychology: Applies psychological principles to the workplace. Employee selection, training, motivation, leadership, job satisfaction, organisational culture, performance appraisal.
  • Educational Psychology: Focuses on learning processes, teaching methods, curriculum design, special education, and assessment. School psychologists work with children having learning disabilities or behavioural issues.
  • Health Psychology: Examines how biological, psychological, and social factors influence health and illness. Stress management, health promotion, coping with chronic illness, adherence to medical advice.
  • Forensic Psychology: Intersection of psychology and the legal system. Criminal profiling, competency evaluations, eyewitness testimony reliability, child custody evaluations, correctional psychology.
  • Sport Psychology: Psychological factors affecting athletic performance, motivation, anxiety, team dynamics, rehabilitation from injury.
  • Environmental Psychology: Interaction between humans and their physical environment; effects of noise, crowding, architecture, and nature on behaviour and well‑being.
  • Neuropsychology: Relationship between brain function and behaviour. Works with patients having brain injuries, strokes, or neurological disorders.

Exam Tip: Questions often ask to differentiate between clinical and counselling psychology, or to explain the scope of any two branches. Always mention the focus area, typical clients/issues, and work settings.

📌 Unit 3: Schools of Psychology

▼

3.1 Structuralism

Founded by Wilhelm Wundt and developed by his student Edward Titchener at Cornell University. Structuralism aimed to analyse the structure of conscious experience by breaking it down into its basic elements: sensations, images, and feelings. The primary method was introspection – trained observers would carefully report their conscious experiences in response to controlled stimuli.

Criticism: Introspection was highly subjective and unreliable; different observers often reported different experiences. The approach was also criticised for being too narrow and ignoring the function of consciousness.

3.2 Functionalism

William James, heavily influenced by Darwin's theory of natural selection, argued that psychology should focus on the function of consciousness rather than its structure. Functionalism asked: "What is the purpose of this behaviour? How does it help the organism adapt?" This approach emphasised real‑world applications, individual differences, and the continuity of mental life (James famously described consciousness as a "stream").

Functionalism paved the way for behaviourism and applied psychology. It also encouraged the study of animals, children, and abnormal behaviour – groups largely ignored by structuralists.

3.3 Behaviourism

John B. Watson (founder) and later B.F. Skinner rejected the study of consciousness entirely, arguing that psychology should focus only on observable, measurable behaviour. Key principles: stimulus‑response (S‑R) psychology, reinforcement, and punishment. Watson famously claimed he could take any infant and train them to become any type of specialist.

The Little Albert experiment (Watson & Rayner, 1920) demonstrated that fear could be classically conditioned in a human infant. Skinner extended behaviourism with operant conditioning, showing how consequences shape voluntary behaviour. Behaviourism dominated American psychology for nearly 50 years.

3.4 Psychoanalysis

Sigmund Freud developed psychoanalysis, which emphasises the role of the unconscious mind, childhood experiences, and psychosexual stages. According to Freud, personality has three structures: Id (pleasure principle, present from birth), Ego (reality principle, develops around age 2‑3), and Superego (morality, develops around age 5).

Defence mechanisms (repression, projection, denial, rationalisation) protect the ego from anxiety. Freud's methods included free association, dream analysis, and analysis of transference. Though many of Freud's specific ideas have been criticised, his emphasis on the unconscious and early experience remains influential.

3.5 Humanistic Psychology

Emerging in the 1950s‑60s as a "third force" (after behaviourism and psychoanalysis), humanistic psychology was pioneered by Carl Rogers and Abraham Maslow. It emphasises free will, personal growth, and the inherent goodness of people. Rogers proposed unconditional positive regard as essential for healthy development and developed client‑centred therapy. Maslow proposed the hierarchy of needs, culminating in self‑actualization.

3.6 Cognitive Psychology

Emerging in the 1960s as a response to behaviourism's limitations, cognitive psychology reintroduced the scientific study of mental processes. The "cognitive revolution" was fuelled by advances in computer science and information processing. Key figures: Ulric Neisser (who coined "cognitive psychology"), Jean Piaget (cognitive development), Noam Chomsky (language). Focus: memory, perception, attention, language, problem‑solving, decision‑making.

Mnemonic: "S‑F‑B‑P‑H‑C" — Chronological order of schools:

Structuralism → Functionalism → Behaviourism → Psychoanalysis → Humanistic → Cognitive

Exam Tip: Comparative questions are extremely common: "Compare behaviourism and psychoanalysis." or "How did cognitive psychology differ from behaviourism?" Always mention: key proponents, core focus, methods used, and major criticisms for each school.

📝 Solved Previous Year Questions (PYQs) – BPCC 101

📌 Section A – Short Answer Questions (2‑5 Marks)

Q1. Define psychology. 2 MARKS

Answer: Psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes. It encompasses both observable actions (what people do) and internal mental experiences (thoughts, emotions, memories). The term derives from Greek psyche (soul/mind) and logos (study/discourse). Modern psychology is both a natural and social science, using empirical methods to understand and predict behaviour.

Q2. What is classical conditioning? 5 MARKS

Answer: Classical conditioning, discovered by Ivan Pavlov, is a form of associative learning where a neutral stimulus becomes associated with a meaningful stimulus and acquires the capacity to elicit a similar response. Key elements: Unconditioned Stimulus (UCS) – e.g., food that naturally elicits a response; Unconditioned Response (UCR) – e.g., salivation to food; Conditioned Stimulus (CS) – initially neutral bell that becomes associated; Conditioned Response (CR) – salivation to bell alone. Principles include acquisition, extinction, spontaneous recovery, stimulus generalisation, and discrimination. Watson and Rayner demonstrated conditioned emotional responses in the Little Albert experiment.

Q3. Explain Maslow's hierarchy of needs. 5 MARKS

Answer: Abraham Maslow proposed a five‑tier motivational model in the shape of a pyramid. From bottom to top: Physiological needs (food, water, sleep) are the most basic. Safety needs (security, stability) come next. Love and belongingness (relationships, community) follow. Esteem needs (achievement, respect) are higher. At the apex is Self‑actualization – fulfilling one's potential. Maslow argued that lower needs must be substantially satisfied before higher needs become motivating. The theory is widely applied in education, management, and therapy, though criticised for being culture‑bound (reflecting Western values).

Q4. Differentiate between sensation and perception. 3 MARKS

Answer: Sensation is the process by which sensory receptors and sense organs detect and receive physical energy from the environment (light, sound, pressure) and convert it into neural signals. It is bottom‑up processing. Perception is the active process of organising, interpreting, and making meaningful sense of that sensory input. It involves top‑down processing using prior knowledge, expectations, and context. Example: Sensation is detecting light patterns on the retina; perception is recognising a friend's face. Sensation provides the raw data; perception gives it meaning.

Q5. What is working memory? 3 MARKS

Answer: Baddeley and Hitch's (1974) working memory model replaced the concept of a unitary short‑term store with a multi‑component system. It includes: Central executive – controls attention, integrates information; Phonological loop – processes verbal/auditory information (subdivided into phonological store and articulatory rehearsal process); Visuospatial sketchpad – handles visual and spatial information; and the Episodic buffer (added later) – integrates information from different sources into coherent episodes and links to long‑term memory. Working memory emphasises active manipulation rather than passive storage.

📌 Section B – Long Answer Questions (10‑15 Marks)

Q6. Compare and contrast any two schools of psychology. 10 MARKS

Answer: Behaviourism (Watson, Skinner) and Psychoanalysis (Freud) represent sharply different approaches.

Focus: Behaviourism studies only observable behaviour, rejecting mental processes as unscientific. Psychoanalysis focuses on unconscious drives, conflicts, and childhood experiences.

Determinism: Behaviourism emphasises environmental determinism – behaviour is shaped by conditioning and reinforcement. Psychoanalysis emphasises biological/instinctual determinism – behaviour is driven by unconscious libidinal and aggressive impulses.

Methods: Behaviourists use controlled laboratory experiments (e.g., Skinner box, Little Albert). Psychoanalysts rely on clinical methods: free association, dream analysis, and case studies.

View of human nature: Behaviourism sees humans as blank slates shaped entirely by experience. Psychoanalysis views humans as driven by primitive urges that must be tamed by society.

Contributions: Behaviourism gave psychology scientific rigour, effective therapies (behaviour modification, CBT), and practical applications in education. Psychoanalysis highlighted the importance of the unconscious, early experience, and defence mechanisms.

Criticisms: Behaviourism oversimplifies human experience, ignoring cognition and emotion. Psychoanalysis lacks empirical testability and overemphasises sexuality.

Q7. Describe the Atkinson‑Shiffrin model of memory. Discuss its strengths and limitations. 10 MARKS

Answer: The Atkinson‑Shiffrin model (1968), also called the multi‑store model, proposes three distinct memory stores:

1. Sensory Memory: Briefly retains sensory information in its original form (iconic for visual, echoic for auditory). Large capacity but very brief duration (iconic <1 second, echoic 3‑4 seconds). Only information that is attended to moves forward.

2. Short‑Term Memory (STM): Limited capacity (7±2 chunks, per Miller) and limited duration (approximately 20‑30 seconds without rehearsal). Information can be maintained through maintenance rehearsal.

3. Long‑Term Memory (LTM): Potentially unlimited capacity and permanent duration. Information enters LTM through elaborative rehearsal and consolidation.

Strengths: Provides a clear, testable framework. Inspired vast amounts of research. Distinguishes between different types of memory stores. Explains the serial position effect (primacy effect = LTM, recency effect = STM).

Limitations: Oversimplifies STM as a passive, unitary store (led to working memory model). Overemphasises rehearsal as the route to LTM – we often remember things without deliberate rehearsal. Does not fully account for different types of LTM (episodic, semantic, procedural). The model is linear and does not capture the interactive, parallel nature of memory processing.

Q8. Discuss Piaget's stages of cognitive development with appropriate examples. 10 MARKS

Answer: Jean Piaget proposed that children progress through four invariant, universal stages of cognitive development, each characterised by qualitatively different ways of thinking:

1. Sensorimotor Stage (Birth to 2 years): Infants understand the world through sensory experiences and motor actions. Key achievement: object permanence – understanding that objects continue to exist even when out of sight (develops around 8‑12 months). Example: A toy hidden under a blanket is sought by the child.

2. Preoperational Stage (2 to 7 years): Children use symbols (language, pretend play) but lack logical operations. Characteristics: egocentrism (difficulty seeing others' perspectives – three mountains task), animism (attributing life to inanimate objects), centration (focusing on one aspect while ignoring others), and lack of conservation (understanding that quantity remains the same despite changes in appearance – e.g., liquid in different shaped glasses).

3. Concrete Operational Stage (7 to 11 years): Children can think logically about concrete events. Achievements: conservation (all types – liquid, number, mass, volume), reversibility (understanding that operations can be reversed), classification (grouping objects into hierarchies), seriation (ordering objects along a dimension). However, they struggle with abstract, hypothetical reasoning.

4. Formal Operational Stage (11 years and beyond): Adolescents and adults can think abstractly, hypothetically, and systematically. Abilities: hypothetico‑deductive reasoning (formulating and testing hypotheses), propositional thought (evaluating logic of statements independent of content), metacognition (thinking about thinking). Example: Solving algebraic equations, considering hypothetical moral dilemmas.

Piaget's theory revolutionised developmental psychology, though critics note that he underestimated children's abilities and that development is more continuous and culturally influenced than he proposed.
