📌 Unit 1: Introduction to Psychology

▼

1.1 Meaning and Definition

Psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes. The term comes from Greek psyche (soul) and logos (study). Modern psychology focuses on observable behaviour and internal mental states.

Mnemonic: "ABC" — Core of Psychology: Affect (emotions), Behaviour (actions), Cognition (thoughts)

1.2 Goals of Psychology

  1. Describe: Observe and record behaviour.
  2. Explain: Understand causes.
  3. Predict: Forecast behaviour.
  4. Control/Modify: Influence behaviour positively.

🔶 Important Questions

  1. Define psychology. Explain its goals.
  2. Discuss psychology as a science.

📌 Unit 2: Branches of Psychology

▼

2.1 Major Branches

  • Cognitive Psychology: Thinking, memory, language.
  • Developmental Psychology: Changes across lifespan.
  • Clinical Psychology: Mental disorders and therapy.
  • Social Psychology: Individual in social context.
  • Industrial/Organizational: Workplace behaviour.
  • Educational Psychology: Learning processes.

🔶 Important Questions

  1. Describe the major branches of psychology.
  2. How does social psychology differ from clinical psychology?

📌 Unit 3: Schools of Psychology

▼

3.1 Early Schools

  • Structuralism (Wundt, Titchener): Analyze consciousness into basic elements via introspection.
  • Functionalism (William James): Function of consciousness; adaptation.
  • Behaviourism (Watson, Skinner): Study of observable behaviour; stimulus-response.
  • Psychoanalysis (Freud): Unconscious mind, childhood experiences.
  • Humanistic (Maslow, Rogers): Free will, self-actualization.

Exam Tip: Compare any two schools—often Structuralism vs. Functionalism or Behaviourism vs. Psychoanalysis.

🔶 Important Questions

  1. Critically evaluate behaviourism as a school of psychology.
  2. Differentiate between structuralism and functionalism.

📌 Unit 4: Methods of Psychological Enquiry

▼

4.1 Key Methods

  1. Observation: Naturalistic, participant, systematic.
  2. Experimental: Independent/dependent variables, control group.
  3. Survey: Questionnaires, interviews.
  4. Case Study: In-depth analysis of a single case.
  5. Correlational: Relationship between variables.

🔶 Important Questions

  1. Explain the experimental method. Discuss its strengths and limitations.
  2. What is a case study? When is it used?

▼

5.1 Nervous System & Brain

Neurons, synapses, central (brain, spinal cord) and peripheral nervous system. Major brain parts: cerebrum, cerebellum, brainstem. Endocrine system: glands and hormones.

🔶 Important Questions

📌 Unit 6: Sensory & Perceptual Processes

▼

6.1 Sensation vs. Perception

Sensation: Detection of stimuli by sense organs. Perception: Interpretation of sensory input. Gestalt principles of organization (figure-ground, proximity, closure).

🔶 Important Questions

  1. Differentiate between sensation and perception.
  2. Explain Gestalt laws of perceptual organization.

📌 Unit 7: Learning

▼

7.1 Classical Conditioning

Pavlov: UCS, UCR, CS, CR. Little Albert experiment (Watson). Principles: acquisition, extinction, generalization.

7.2 Operant Conditioning

Skinner: Reinforcement (positive/negative), punishment. Schedules of reinforcement.

7.3 Observational Learning

Bandura's Bobo doll experiment: attention, retention, reproduction, motivation.

🔶 Important Questions

  1. Explain classical conditioning with an example.
  2. Compare classical and operant conditioning.

📌 Unit 8: Memory

▼

8.1 Models of Memory

Atkinson-Shiffrin model: Sensory memory → Short-term (STM) → Long-term (LTM). Working memory (Baddeley). Levels of processing (Craik & Lockhart).

8.2 Forgetting

Decay, interference (proactive/retroactive), retrieval failure. Ebbinghaus forgetting curve.

🔶 Important Questions

  1. Explain the Atkinson-Shiffrin model of memory.
  2. What are the major causes of forgetting?

📌 Unit 9: Motivation & Emotion

▼

9.1 Theories of Motivation

  • Drive Reduction (Hull): Biological needs create drives.
  • Maslow's Hierarchy: Physiological → Safety → Love/Belonging → Esteem → Self-actualization.
  • Expectancy Theory: Effort → Performance → Reward.

9.2 Emotion

James-Lange theory: Physiological arousal → emotion. Cannon-Bard: Simultaneous. Schachter-Singer: Arousal + cognitive label.

🔶 Important Questions

  1. Explain Maslow's hierarchy of needs.
  2. Compare James-Lange and Cannon-Bard theories of emotion.

📌 Unit 10: Intelligence & Aptitude

▼

10.1 Definitions & Theories

Spearman's g-factor, Thurstone's primary mental abilities, Gardner's multiple intelligences, Sternberg's triarchic theory. IQ formula: MA/CA × 100. Types of intelligence tests: Binet, Wechsler scales.

10.2 Aptitude vs Intelligence

Aptitude is potential for specific skills; intelligence is general cognitive ability.

🔶 Important Questions

  1. Discuss Gardner's theory of multiple intelligences.
  2. Differentiate between intelligence and aptitude.

📝 Solved Previous Year Questions (PYQs) – BPCC 101

▼

Short Answer Questions

Q1. Define psychology. 2 MARKS

Answer: Psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes. It derives from Greek psyche (soul) and logos (study).

Q2. What is classical conditioning? 5 MARKS

Answer: Classical conditioning, discovered by Ivan Pavlov, is learning through association. A neutral stimulus (e.g., bell) paired with an unconditioned stimulus (food) elicits a conditioned response (salivation). Key elements: UCS, UCR, CS, CR.

Q3. Explain Maslow's hierarchy of needs. 5 MARKS

Answer: Abraham Maslow proposed a five-tier pyramid: Physiological (food, water), Safety, Love/Belonging, Esteem, Self-actualization. Lower needs must be satisfied before higher ones motivate behaviour.

Long Answer Questions

Q4. Compare and contrast any two schools of psychology. 10 MARKS

Answer: Behaviourism (Watson, Skinner) focuses on observable behaviour, stimulus-response, and environmental determinism; it rejects introspection. Psychoanalysis (Freud) emphasizes unconscious processes, childhood conflicts, and internal drives. While behaviourism is objective and experimental, psychoanalysis relies on clinical case studies and symbolic interpretation.

Q5. Discuss the Atkinson-Shiffrin model of memory. 10 MARKS

Answer: The model proposes three stages: Sensory memory (large capacity, brief duration), Short-term memory/STM (limited 7±2 items, 20-30 sec), and Long-term memory/LTM (unlimited capacity, permanent). Information flows through attention and rehearsal. The model spurred research but oversimplifies memory as a passive store.
